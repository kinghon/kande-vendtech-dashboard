Most people don’t struggle in vending because they lack effort.

They struggle because they try to move forward before they have a steady base to stand on.

This module is about slowing things down just enough to get oriented — so the rest of the work feels clearer, calmer, and more repeatable.

If you’re brand new, this is where you build the mental and operational foundation that makes everything else possible.
If you’ve already launched, this is where you can spot which basics might be creating friction later without you realizing it.

Nothing here needs to be perfect.
The goal is to set things up once, in a way that supports consistent action instead of constant second-guessing.

As you move through this module:

Focus on clarity, not optimization

Notice where you’re adding pressure that doesn’t need to be there

Let this be about stability, not speed

If you ever feel unsure whether you’re “doing enough,” that’s normal — and it’s part of this stage. Use the lessons to ground yourself, and bring questions into the comments or the Ops Spaces when something doesn’t quite click.

Takeaways

Feeling unsure at the beginning is normal — it’s not a sign you’re behind.

Confidence grows through steady action, not perfect decisions.

Consistency matters more than talent in this business.

Simple systems make vending repeatable and less stressful.

Early momentum comes from staying calm and showing up.

How to Make Product Mix Changes without Thrashing
Modern Amenities
⚡️
Feb 2, 2026
Why this exists

At some point after launch, you hit this strange phase: things are selling… but it’s not obvious why.

A few items fly. A few sit there forever. And every time you open the machine, you feel the urge to “fix” the mix.

This Space exists because merchandising is one of the easiest places to accidentally create chaos. Not because you’re doing something wrong — but because it’s very easy to change a lot, very quickly, before the machine has shown you a real pattern.

When to use this

You’re in the right place if you’re thinking any of these things:

“Should I pull this item, or give it more time?”


“Why does this sell in one location and flop in another?”


“I keep changing the mix but I’m not sure if I’m actually improving anything.”


“I want a smarter way to test without constantly second-guessing myself.”


How to use this Space

Use this Space like a decision helper, not a masterclass.

Start with the checklist below to get clear on what kind of situation you’re actually in. Then make one small move that matches that situation — instead of changing five things and hoping it helps.

If you’re here looking for product ideas, you’ll also find a community thread for that. The goal there is to share ideas by context (type of location, who’s using the machine, what the foot traffic feels like), not to trade generic “top sellers.”

Before You Change Anything: A Quick Checklist

Most product mix mistakes aren’t bad products. They’re changing too much, too fast, before the machine has shown you a real signal.

Run through this before you swap anything.

1) Am I reacting to a pattern or a moment?

A simple way to tell:

If you’ve only noticed it for a few days (or one refill), that’s usually a moment.


If you’ve noticed the same thing happening across multiple visits, it’s closer to a pattern.


You don’t need perfect data here. You just need repetition.

2) Has this item had a fair shot in this location?

Instead of asking “how many transactions,” ask:

Has it been in stock and visible most of the time?


Has it been there through at least two different types of days?
(For example: weekday vs weekend, or a normal week vs a slower or busier stretch.)


If it’s been out of stock, hidden, or constantly moved, it hasn’t really had a fair shot yet.

3) Am I changing this because sales are weak — or because I’m annoyed?

This one matters more than people like to admit.

If the machine is performing fine overall but one item keeps bothering you, you might be solving discomfort, not performance.

A good rule:
Only change things that have a clear reason attached.

4) Did I recently change more than one thing?

If yes, slow down.

When you change products, prices, placement, depth, and neighboring items all at once, you make it very hard to tell what caused the result.

One change at a time is boring — but it’s how you actually learn faster.

5) If I replace this item, do I know what question I’m trying to answer?

This is the most important one.

Examples of a clear question:

“Do people want a better version of the same thing?”


“Is the issue price, or is it the category?”


“Is this slot better used for a repeat staple than a novelty test?”


If you can’t name the question, pause. Don’t swap just to swap.

6) What does “better inventory” mean for this machine?

An item is “better” if it reliably does at least one of these:

Sells steadily (not necessarily fast — just predictably)


Earns more per slot


Creates repeat behavior


Solves a real use-case in that location (late-night hunger, quick drink, something kids ask for, etc.)


Pairs naturally with something else people already buy
(for example: a drink + a snack, or a meal item + something sweet)


Before pulling something, decide which of those you’re aiming for.

7) Make the smallest move that answers your question

Instead of a full reset, you can often learn more with a smaller change:

Adjust depth (not just to sell more, but to see whether demand is being constrained by stock-outs)


Adjust placement (more visible vs less visible)


Adjust adjacency (avoiding cannibalization)


Keep the item but change its role (staple vs test)


Adding depth, in particular, is often less about increasing sales immediately and more about confirming whether an item deserves to be treated as a true staple.

Optional: write one line so you don’t rely on memory

When you make a change, write a single sentence:

“I changed ___ because I’m testing whether ___.”

That’s it. That alone prevents a lot of churn.

What operators tend to realize later

These aren’t rules. They’re patterns that show up again and again once machines have real history.

Many operators later realize they weren’t learning as much as they thought — not because they changed things, but because they changed too many things too quickly.

The difference is intent: early rotation works best when you’re clear about what you’re testing and why.

Consistency beats cleverness more often than people expect

Not because novelty never works — but because repeat behavior pays the bills. Familiar items reduce friction. “Boring” products often carry the machine.

Tests still happen. They’re just smaller and more intentional.

Location matters more than taste

What sells is rarely about what you would buy. It’s about who’s walking by, when they’re there, and what problem they’re trying to solve in that moment.

The same product can be a hero in one place and invisible in another — with no lesson beyond context matters.

Over-adjusting creates more confusion than under-adjusting

Many operators go through an early phase of changing products, prices, and placement all at once. What usually follows isn’t improvement — it’s uncertainty.

Once changes slow down, patterns get easier to see.

When the mix is working, it often feels calm

Fewer second guesses. Fewer impulse swaps. Less “maybe I should…”

This Space exists for one reason:

To help you stop making the same inventory decisions over and over again, just under slightly different circumstances.

If ordering still feels busy, stressful, or oddly emotional, you’re not doing anything wrong.
You’ve simply reached a stage where deciding more deliberately matters more than working harder.

You’re in the right place if…

ordering takes more time than it should


you’re reacting to sales but not feeling confident about your decisions


inventory choices are competing with higher-value work


If any of that feels familiar, this Space is meant to help.

A simple truth to anchor everything here

You can’t sell what isn’t there.

Stockouts cost revenue.
Overbuying costs attention and cash.

Ordering discipline is the practice of protecting revenue without turning inventory into a constant mental drain — by learning to respond to patterns, not moments.

What this Space is (and isn’t)

This is:

a way to think more clearly about ordering


language for decisions you’re already making


a place to reduce noise and regain control


This is not:

a SKU list


a forecasting model


a “best inventory setup”


a promise that everything will sell eventually


There are bad fits.
There are products worth pulling.
The goal here is learning when and why, not avoiding change.

Why inventory often feels harder after launch

Early on, many operators:

overvalue variety


undervalue depth


restock everything “just in case”


treat every sale as a signal to act


That’s normal.

Stress usually appears when you realize you’re spending a lot of time ordering — but still don’t feel confident you’re protecting revenue.

What “ordering discipline” actually means

In practice, it looks like this:

Protecting bestsellers first
Your primary job is to prevent repeat stockouts, not to keep everything equally full.


Letting slow movers reveal themselves
Pulling something too quickly is common. Holding something too long is also common.
Single weeks can be misleading — repeated behavior is what matters.


Not every visit needs to trigger new decisions
Discipline often shows up as fewer decisions per visit, not better ones.


Separating feeding the machine from testing
Core products exist to keep revenue stable.
Test products should be limited, intentional, and reviewed separately.


At its core, ordering discipline means learning to respond to patterns, not moments.

Common inflection points operators notice

Many operators eventually recognize moments like these:

“I kept adding new products and flavors because I thought the machine needed more variety — but the real problem was that my bestsellers didn’t have enough depth, so they kept selling out.”


“Every visit produced a long picklist because items showed up after a single sale, even when there wasn’t real stockout risk — so I was reacting to noise instead of focusing on what actually needed attention.”


“I was restocking as soon as something sold down because I was worried about running out, even though the pattern didn’t actually justify a visit yet.”


These aren’t failures.
They’re signs that your business is stabilizing and revealing where structure will help.

A note on stockouts (important)

Stockouts will happen.
Demand isn’t perfectly smooth.

A single stockout tells you something happened.
Repeated stockouts tell you something needs to change.

The goal isn’t perfection — it’s response:

adding depth


reducing unnecessary variety


adjusting minimums


rethinking placement


Calm comes from learning to respond to patterns, not moments, instead of reacting to isolated incidents.

What changes when discipline clicks

Operators often notice:

fewer emergency runs


clearer picklists


more predictable visits


more confidence in reorders


better use of distributor ordering


Most importantly, inventory stops consuming the time and attention you need for the rest of the business.

You don’t need to fix everything at once.
The first win is noticing where you’re reacting instead of responding.

This Space exists to help you develop a steady, practical response to the kinds of issues that show up once machines are live: mechanical wear, software alerts, connectivity hiccups, temperature warnings, and the occasional truly frustrating failure.

If something has already gone wrong — or you’re worried about what might — you’re not behind.

You’re operating real equipment in real environments.

You’re in the right place if…

maintenance interruptions keep pulling you off your plan


you’re unsure how to establish a reasonable maintenance routine


issues feel disruptive even when they’re handled


reliability takes up more mental space than it should


This Space is about restoring perspective — and protecting your time.

A simple truth to anchor everything here

Reliability is not the absence of problems.
It’s how predictably you handle the ones that arise.

Whether you’re running smart machines, traditional machines, or a mix of both, the underlying challenge is the same: learning how urgently, how often, and in what way a problem needs to be addressed.

Good maintenance reduces avoidable stress.

What this Space is (and isn’t)

This is:

guidance for responding to problems proportionally


help establishing a sane, repeatable maintenance rhythm


context for what’s normal — and what isn’t


reassurance grounded in real operator experience


This is not:

a repair manual


machine-specific troubleshooting


an excuse to ignore real problems


a requirement to fix everything yourself


Problems still get solved here.
The goal is solving them without constant disruption.

What “maintenance” looks like in practice

Maintenance shows up differently depending on the machines you run, but the decision patterns are similar.

For smart machines, stress often comes from:

offline or connectivity alerts


payment interruptions


temperature or health-and-safety warnings


notifications triggered after restocking


dashboards surfacing issues that don’t always impact sales


For traditional machines, it may look like:

bill validator or coin mech issues


motors or spirals misbehaving


door or latch wear


environment-related problems (dust, humidity, heat)


In both cases, the key skill is learning how to respond calmly and proportionally — not dismissing problems, but not escalating everything either.

Deciding how to respond

Some problems require immediate correction.
Others are best handled by adjusting routines, settings, or habits over time.

A useful frame many operators adopt:

One failure is information


Repeated failures are instruction


Frequency, consistency, and impact matter more than a single incident.

Accepting what you can’t control — without giving up

Early failures can feel especially unfair:

a component fails weeks in


a machine goes offline longer than expected


shipping timelines don’t cooperate


Most operators experience this at some point.

The shift is learning to:

advocate clearly and respectfully with support teams


document issues and timelines


use warranties when available


accept that some downtime is part of the business


Calm doesn’t come from pretending it’s fine.
It comes from knowing you’re doing the right things.

Establishing a reasonable maintenance routine

Early on, it’s hard to know what “normal” looks like.

Questions like:

How often should I clean components?


When does preventative maintenance become overkill?


What actually needs regular attention in this environment?


These don’t have universal answers.

That’s where community experience — and Spaces like this — matter.
Over time, most operators find a rhythm that keeps machines dependable without turning maintenance into a constant task.

A real note on vandalism

Vandalism is rare — until it isn’t.

Some operators experience:

broken doors or glass


cut latches


stolen components


repeated incidents at the same location


When it happens, it’s costly and disruptive.

Many experienced operators now:

discuss vandalism explicitly with property management


include contract language addressing repair cost recovery


make intentional decisions about insurance, deductibles, and risk


There’s no single right approach — but planning beats surprise.

What changes when reliability becomes predictable

Operators often notice:

fewer emergency responses


clearer judgment about urgency


more productive conversations with manufacturers and support


confidence in delaying non-urgent fixes


less background stress


Most importantly, machines stop feeling fragile — and start feeling manageable.

You don’t need to prevent every problem.
The first win is establishing a routine that handles issues without consuming your attention.

Thinking About Financing With Intention

It’s easy to start with the payment.

The rate.
The term.
The approval odds.

But most operators who feel good about their financing decisions didn’t start there. They started by getting honest about the opportunity — and about themselves.

Financing isn’t just math. It’s stress level. It’s flexibility. It’s how you’ll feel in month two if sales are slower than expected.

This guide isn’t here to scare you or slow you down unnecessarily.

It’s here to help you move forward with clarity.

Step 1: Be Clear About What Financing Is For

Before comparing options or speaking with partners, pause and ask:

Do I feel confident in this location — or just hopeful?

If sales ramp slower than expected, would I still feel okay carrying this payment?

Am I financing to move faster — or because I don’t have another option?

You don’t need perfect prediction. No one has that — especially on a first machine.

But you should have a reasoned belief based on:

Real foot traffic observation

Fit with the property

Conservative math

Honest conversations

Financing works best when it supports thoughtful conviction — not pressure.

Step 2: Look at the Whole Monthly Picture

Instead of asking:

Can I afford the payment?

Try asking:

If sales are 20–30% lower than I expect, does this still work?

Think through everything you’ll actually pay each month:

Machine payment

Card processing

Software/subscription fees

Connectivity

Product cost

Revenue share (if applicable)

Taxes

If this is your first placement, your projections will naturally include uncertainty. That’s normal.

The goal isn’t perfect accuracy.
The goal is avoiding fragile math that only works in the best-case scenario.

If you’re expanding from a strong-performing machine, your focus shifts toward efficiency — but the full picture still matters.

Financing should support a location you believe in — not create pressure to prove yourself right.

Step 3: Work Through This Honestly (Write It Down)

Before speaking with any financing partner, grab a notebook or open a document and jot down your answers to the questions below.

Don’t overanalyze. Don’t try to impress anyone. Just answer honestly.

This is for you.

Time in Business

How long has my business been generating revenue?

Do I have documented income yet?

Have I completed at least one full month of operation?

Some financing structures rely on business history. Others are designed specifically for newer operators.

Knowing where you stand keeps conversations productive.

Personal Credit

Do I know my current credit score?

Is my credit utilization high?

Would a hard pull matter right now?

Some structures lean heavily on personal credit.
Some minimize impact.
Some include credit strategy conversations.

Clarity here reduces surprises.

Revenue Stability

Is this my first machine?

Or am I expanding from a location that’s already proven itself?

Have I seen enough sales to understand my average week?

Financing a first machine carries more unknowns than financing an expansion.

That doesn’t make it wrong — it just means your margin for error is smaller.

Cushion

Do I have savings beyond the down payment?

Could I carry the payment if month one is slower than expected?

This question alone prevents a lot of regret.

Expansion Timing

Will this be my only payment for now?

Or am I planning to add another machine soon?

If two locations ramp at different speeds, am I comfortable carrying both payments at once?

Adding machines can absolutely make sense when the math is clear.

Just be mindful of timing. New locations don’t always stabilize at the same pace. Planning for that reduces stress.

Step 4: Understand Structure — Not Just Approval

Financing options differ in more than just rates.

They vary in:

How much they rely on personal credit

Whether time in business matters

Whether underwriting looks at revenue

Whether there are hard or soft pulls

How early payoff works

Total repayment over time

It’s tempting to focus on speed or ease of approval.

But approval doesn’t mean it’s the right structure for you.

Ask about:

Total repayment

Flexibility

What happens if you want to pay it off early

How this affects your ability to fund another machine later

You’re not just financing equipment.

You’re shaping your risk exposure.

Step 5: Match Structure to Where You Actually Are

After working through the checklist, look at financing partners through that lens.

Some structures are built primarily around strong personal credit.

Some are designed to help newer operators without long time in business.

Some focus on credit structuring strategies.

Some rely more heavily on documented revenue.

There isn’t a single “best” option.

There’s alignment.

Traditional banks often require established history. Many operators in earlier stages explore alternative structures that better match where they are.

Choose the structure that fits your situation — not simply the one that says yes first.

Approval is not the same as readiness.

Step 6: Talk It Through

Financing decisions are easier to think through out loud.

Use:

Ambassador calls

Partner replay calls

The main feed

When asking for input, share:

Location type

Conservative revenue estimate

Machine cost

Everything you expect to pay each month

Estimated financing payment

Time in business

Whether this is first placement or expansion

Specific details lead to grounded feedback.

General questions produce vague answers.

What This Guide Is Designed to Prevent

Rushing into financing without thinking through exposure

Confusing approval with confidence

Building projections around best-case scenarios

Expanding faster than your cushion allows

You don’t need perfect certainty.

You need awareness.

Operators in this community have built successful businesses from many different starting points — strong credit, rebuilding credit, brand new LLCs, years in business, and everything in between.

There isn’t one correct path.

Financing is simply a tool.

When you align the financing structure with your actual situation, it becomes leverage — not pressure.

Slow down.
Write it out.
Have the conversations.
Then move forward deliberately.

Choosing Machines With Intention

It’s easy to start with the machine.

The formats. The finishes. The features.

But strong operators usually begin one layer earlier — with the type of route they’re building and the kind of locations they want to serve.

Machine decisions shape capital exposure and daily workflow. They also shape how properties perceive your business model.

This guide is here to help you slow down just enough to make a clean first decision.

As you read, jot down quick answers to the questions. You don’t need perfect certainty — just enough clarity to move forward intentionally.

Step 1: Decide What Kind of Locations You’re Building Around

Before evaluating a specific property, zoom out.

Ask yourself:

What types of environments am I realistically targeting?

Office? Gym? Residential? Industrial?

Am I aiming for fewer, stronger placements — or a more distributed route?

In many cases, fewer higher-traffic placements create cleaner economics and simpler operations — especially with smart systems.

More distributed routes can absolutely work — but they rely on:

Tight routing discipline

Careful cost control

Clear expectations around volume

Neither model is “better.”

What matters is being intentional about which one you’re stepping into.

If this is your first machine, it’s normal to feel the weight of the decision.
If you’re expanding, it’s normal to feel the shift — this is real growth.

Make sure the opportunity fits within a broader plan — rather than becoming the plan by default.

Step 2: Evaluate the Property — Not Just the Hardware

Before looking at models, clarify:

What traffic patterns are realistically present?

What problem are you solving?

What revenue would justify the capital required?

Avoid:

Letting a property manager select from a catalog

Leading with “Which machine do you want?”

Designing around aesthetics without revenue logic

Strong operators evaluate the property first — then match hardware to it.

If projected revenue doesn’t support the capital required, adjust the hardware — not the math.

You don’t need certainty.
You need reasoning.

Step 3: Don’t Oversize Before the Location Earns It

Sizing mistakes usually happen early — and they’re expensive to unwind.

A few grounded truths:

It’s easier to add capacity later than to shrink it.

If traffic feels unclear, that should influence your capital commitment.

Features should match revenue logic.

On a first placement, estimation is normal.

The goal isn’t to guess perfectly.
The goal is to avoid locking yourself into the biggest option before the location has earned it.

If traffic turns out stronger than expected, upgrading is a good problem.

Step 4: Choose an Ecosystem You Can Operate Confidently

Every machine comes with more than hardware.

It includes:

A backend dashboard

Processing fees

Subscription layers

Reporting systems

Ongoing workflow

Running multiple ecosystems means multiple dashboards, workflows, and fee structures.

For most new operators, choosing one ecosystem and learning it well across several placements reduces unnecessary complexity.

Before committing, ask:

Am I comfortable building my first 3–5 placements inside this system?

Do I understand all the ongoing fees — not just the machine price?

If I place multiple machines, can I manage them cleanly inside this structure?

Early simplicity compounds.

Step 5: Narrow — Then Validate With Real Operators

At some point, research stops being helpful and starts creating noise.

Once you understand your property and capital comfort:

Narrow to 1–2 realistic ecosystems.

Review those systems in VendHub and on manufacturer sites.

Bring focused questions to the community.

Use:

“Meet Fellow Members” to find operators running those systems

Ambassador calls

Event replays

The global search

If you’re torn, that’s normal.

Most operators feel it on their first purchase — and again when they scale.

A few targeted conversations usually make the decision clear.

What This Guide Is Designed to Prevent

Buying based on “cool factor”

Over-sizing before validating demand

Mixing ecosystems without intention

Ignoring ongoing fee layers

Locking into a specific machine too early

Define the route.
Evaluate the property.
Size conservatively when uncertain.
Choose your ecosystem deliberately.
Validate with operators.

You’re going to do this the right way — because you’re not doing it alone.

Then commit.


Where to Continue Your Research

Once you’ve narrowed to 1–2 ecosystems:

Talk with operators running those systems.

You can use “Meet Fellow Members” to find real-world deployments.

Bring focused questions to Ambassador calls.

Review manufacturer and partner pages inside VendHub.

When you’re ready to explore ordering, review current offerings through VendEquipment.

Ordering through the community channel often provides:

Negotiated pricing

VendMarket/Vistar credits (when applicable)

Stronger collective leverage for future discounts

Even if you ultimately choose differently, understanding the ecosystem path first keeps you aligned with community benefits.

Conversations with real operators will always give you more signal than spec sheets alone

Here's how to utilize this template:

-This proposal could be used at a meeting to highlight the full scope of your offer.

-You can send it as a follow-up after a meeting to help close a deal.

-You can omit certain pages for certain scenarios. If you've already settled on a machine with a property, you'd want to omit the page that provides additional machine options. If you're still discussing which machine to place and want to guide them toward the best option, you can keep that page in the proposal.

-For some deals, you may not need the Canva template and you may only need to send the "Scope of Work" proposal template that @Thomas Rohlader has already provided. This is a full proposal template that outlines the details of your deal in depth for your client and there is a spot to include the scope of work document in the Canva template, in case you'd like to pair them together.

Neither of these documents or templates replace the need to send a contract to the client when you are ready to finalize your deal.

In short, customize your proposal for each client and pair these templates together when it is beneficial.

# PART 5: KEY INSIGHTS & TAKEAWAYS FOR KANDE VENDTECH

*Based on course content and community discussions*

## Sales & Location Acquisition

- **Pop-in strategy**: Show up in person (Tue-Thu 10am-2pm), no appointment needed
- **Opening line**: Focus on the "no-cost amenity" angle — they get machines, you handle everything
- **Follow-up**: Most deals close on 3rd+ contact; be systematic, not pushy
- **Best locations**: Apartment complexes (200+ units), office buildings, gyms, car dealerships, warehouses
- **Decision maker**: Property manager, office manager, or HR director depending on location type
- **Common objection handling**: Aesthetics → show branded machine photos; "We already have one" → positioning/reliability angle

## Business Operations

- **Route efficiency**: Group locations geographically to minimize drive time
- **Refill cadence**: Visit based on consumption, not a fixed schedule; target ~70-80% full
- **Product mix**: Start with proven sellers (Gatorade, chips, candy), add location-specific items after data
- **Slow movers**: Pull after 2-3 refill cycles if not selling
- **Pricing**: Office = premium pricing; warehouse/industrial = competitive; apartment = mid-range

## Machine & Inventory Management

- **VendHub**: Use for inventory tracking and route planning from day 1
- **Par levels**: Set per-machine based on traffic patterns
- **Ordering discipline**: Order based on sell-through data, not guesses
- **Maintenance response**: Address issues within 24 hours; keep spare parts on hand
- **Documentation**: Log every visit and maintenance event

## Scaling Strategy

- **Systems first**: Get routes, tracking, and supplier relationships in place before scaling
- **Pace**: Don't add machines faster than you can reliably service them
- **Brand advantage**: Modern Amenities brand supports credibility and premium placement

## Modern Amenities Partnership

- Machine wraps and custom branding differentiate operators
- Direct support line with Kurtis mentioned in course materials
- Kickoff call available to establish working relationship
- Community resources include decision guides, templates, and glossary
