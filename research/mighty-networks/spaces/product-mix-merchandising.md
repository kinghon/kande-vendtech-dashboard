Mar 24, 2026

This deep dive is for operators who like understanding the underlying patterns behind product mix decisions, not just the surface-level guidance.

Optional: Going Deeper (if you're curious)

Good product mix isn't guesswork — it's a set of learnable patterns. These resources go behind the choices you're already making.

Shelf & Planogram Thinking

What it is: Vendor-neutral shelf placement principles from a shopper insights firm, covering assortment focus, signage, and shopper types.
Why it helps vending: Explains why fewer, better-placed products often outperform a crowded slot layout — and how sign placement can quietly block sales.
Link: https://explorerresearch.com/10-principles-for-a-successful-retail-shelf-planogram/

What it is: Industry research report on what c-store shoppers actually buy, when, and why — based on 1,000+ shopper surveys from 2025.
Why it helps vending: Confirms which categories drive impulse purchases and how real pairing patterns (soda + chips, snack + sweet) emerge around time of day.
Link: https://www.acosta.group/the-ongoing-evolution-of-convenience-store-shoppers/

Why People Buy (Behavior Basics)

What it is: A short, clearly written explainer from the St. Louis Fed on how reference prices shape what people are willing to pay.
Why it helps vending: Helps you understand why showing a premium item first can make adjacent items feel like a better deal — useful for slot sequencing and pricing tiers.
Link: https://www.stlouisfed.org/publications/page-one-economics/2021/04/01/the-anchoring-effect

What it is: A 10-minute read on how snack, meal, and pairing behaviors actually play out for c-store shoppers throughout the day.
Why it helps vending: Breaks down real basket-building patterns that translate directly to how you sequence and group SKUs.
Link: https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2023/10/11/c-store-consumer-habits-balancing-impulse-purchases-with-nuanced-usage-occasions/

Testing & Learning Without Thrash

What it is: A convenience-industry feature on the mechanics of impulse purchasing, with category-specific data.
Why it helps vending: Operator-level framing on which product types convert on impulse and which require more deliberate placement work.
Link: https://www.nacsmagazine.com/Issues/May-2023/Understanding-Impulse-Buys

Books (for the nerds)

What it is: A retail anthropologist's field research on how physical environments shape purchase decisions — written for a general audience.
Why it helps vending: Eye-level behavior, grab triggers, and legibility all apply directly to how your machine's shelf gets read in under three seconds.
Link: https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Why-We-Buy/Paco-Underhill/978

How to Make Product Mix Changes without Thrashing
Modern Amenities
⚡️
Feb 2, 2026
Why this exists

At some point after launch, you hit this strange phase: things are selling… but it’s not obvious why.

A few items fly. A few sit there forever. And every time you open the machine, you feel the urge to “fix” the mix.

This Space exists because merchandising is one of the easiest places to accidentally create chaos. Not because you’re doing something wrong — but because it’s very easy to change a lot, very quickly, before the machine has shown you a real pattern.

When to use this

You’re in the right place if you’re thinking any of these things:

“Should I pull this item, or give it more time?”



“Why does this sell in one location and flop in another?”



“I keep changing the mix but I’m not sure if I’m actually improving anything.”



“I want a smarter way to test without constantly second-guessing myself.”



How to use this Space

Use this Space like a decision helper, not a masterclass.

Start with the checklist below to get clear on what kind of situation you’re actually in. Then make one small move that matches that situation — instead of changing five things and hoping it helps.

If you’re here looking for product ideas, you’ll also find a community thread for that. The goal there is to share ideas by context (type of location, who’s using the machine, what the foot traffic feels like), not to trade generic “top sellers.”

Before You Change Anything: A Quick Checklist

Most product mix mistakes aren’t bad products. They’re changing too much, too fast, before the machine has shown you a real signal.

Run through this before you swap anything.

1) Am I reacting to a pattern or a moment?

A simple way to tell:

If you’ve only noticed it for a few days (or one refill), that’s usually a moment.



If you’ve noticed the same thing happening across multiple visits, it’s closer to a pattern.



You don’t need perfect data here. You just need repetition.

2) Has this item had a fair shot in this location?

Instead of asking “how many transactions,” ask:

Has it been in stock and visible most of the time?



Has it been there through at least two different types of days?
(For example: weekday vs weekend, or a normal week vs a slower or busier stretch.)



If it’s been out of stock, hidden, or constantly moved, it hasn’t really had a fair shot yet.

3) Am I changing this because sales are weak — or because I’m annoyed?

This one matters more than people like to admit.

If the machine is performing fine overall but one item keeps bothering you, you might be solving discomfort, not performance.

A good rule:
Only change things that have a clear reason attached.
