If you deleted my entire vending route tomorrow, and forced me to start over, these are the three categories that I would pursue first. Not because others can't work, because these produce predictable behavior, captive demand, repeatable economics. And in vending, predictability is profit.

### 1. Multi-Shift Warehouses (24-Hour Revenue Engine)
- Look for: 150+ employees, 2+ shifts (3 ideal), controlled break windows, limited ability to leave
- Revenue: Weak $1,500/mo, Solid $3,000/mo, High $5,000+ (multiple machines)
- What kills it: Single shift, gas station across street, hidden machines, poor visibility
- Qualifying: How many employees per shift? Are breaks staggered or fixed? Check parking at shift change.
- Decision makers: Operations manager, plant manager, facilities director
- If they oversee multiple sites, ask — one placement can turn into three

### 2. Centralized Residential
- Look for: 250+ units, single building or centralized clubhouse, elevator funnel, mail/package room convergence
- Avoid: Garden style layouts, scattered entrances, no choke points
- Revenue: Under $1,200/mo, Avg $2-3K/mo, Strong $3.5-5K/mo
- Why it works: Impulse + convenience (night, bad weather, sick days, laziness)
- Qualifying: One elevator bank? Where are packages delivered? Stores within walking distance?
- Decision makers: Property manager, regional manager, asset manager

### 3. Outpatient Medical
- Stress + wait time = sales
- Revenue: Conservative $1,200/mo, Solid $1,800/mo, High specialty $2,000/mo
- Top sellers: Smartwater, energy drinks, small snacks, OTC meds
- OTC conversion: Doctor says "Take some Advil" → machine is in the lobby
- What kills it: Hidden placement, fast turnover, easy cafeteria access
- Decision makers: Property manager, office admin, facility manager

### Shared Winning Traits (Score each /10)
1. High daily traffic
2. Captive audience
3. Central visibility
4. Limited alternatives
- Under 30 = pass, Over 32 = investigate aggressively

## Key Takeaways
- All 3 categories share: predictable behavior, captive demand, repeatable economics
- Multi-shift warehouses = most consistent cash flow
- Residential works when traffic is centralized (not garden-style)
- Medical is surprisingly consistent — stress + wait = impulse buys
- Scoring framework removes emotion from placement decisions
