## Draft

A founder asked me last month how long it took before our AI agents "just ran themselves."

Honest answer: they don't.

What they do is remove about 80% of the work from tasks we used to do manually. The remaining 20% — edge cases, judgment calls, situations our agents weren't designed for — still needs a human. And identifying *which* 20% is the hardest part of the job.

We've been running a production multi-agent system for over a year. Scout handles lead research. Relay manages outreach. Piper handles content. Ralph handles engineering QA. They share data, catch each other's errors, and hand off work on defined triggers.

What they don't do: make judgment calls about a lead that doesn't fit the template. Handle a prospect relationship that's gone sideways in a way no rule predicted. Decide that a strategy that's been working for six months needs to change.

Those are still human calls.

The businesses getting the most out of AI automation aren't the ones who handed everything to an agent and walked away. They're the ones who figured out which 80% was worth automating, and then stayed close enough to the 20% to actually catch it when it mattered.

That's the design question we help answer.

**What's the 20% you've kept for yourself after building out automation — and what surprised you about where the line ended up?**
