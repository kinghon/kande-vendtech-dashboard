**Topic:** "How to Hire for an AI-Ready Operations Team" — what changes when agents handle routine work

**Voice:** Kurtis — operator perspective, real experience

---

We've been running production AI agents for over a year now. Here's something nobody warned us about:

The job descriptions changed.

When AI agents handle prospecting, email sequences, content drafts, and data enrichment — the human roles that remain aren't "do the work." They're "decide what to do with the output."

Our team doesn't write outreach emails anymore. They review pipeline reports and decide who gets a phone call. They don't research prospects. They validate what the agents found and add the judgment layer.

That shifts what you're hiring for:

**Before AI agents:**
→ "Can you write 50 cold emails a week?"
→ "Are you good at data entry?"
→ "Can you research companies on LinkedIn?"

**After AI agents:**
→ "Can you look at 50 agent-qualified leads and pick the 5 worth calling?"
→ "Can you spot when the AI output is wrong?"
→ "Can you design the handoff — when does AI stop and you start?"

Three things we look for now:

1. **Pattern recognition over production speed.** The agents produce fast. Humans need to filter fast.

2. **Comfort with imperfect inputs.** Agent output is 85% right, not 100%. The hire who freezes when data isn't perfect won't survive.

3. **Process thinking.** The best people on an AI-augmented team aren't the ones who work hardest — they're the ones who notice when the process is broken and fix the workflow, not just the output.

This isn't theoretical. We learned it by running the system and watching where humans added value vs. where they duplicated what agents already did.

The punchline: AI doesn't eliminate roles. It changes what "good" looks like in every role it touches.

If you're building a team that works alongside AI agents, hire for judgment — not throughput.
