## Draft

75% of businesses plan to deploy AI agents by end of 2026. Under 15% of enterprise AI agent pilots reach production.

That gap isn't a technology problem.

We've spent the last 18 months running AI agents in production — not demos, not pilots, not proof-of-concepts. The ones that survived to month 12 looked completely different from what we built in month 1.

Here's what we learned about the 85%:

**Most pilots are scoped for success, not for reality.** They run on clean data, predictable inputs, and forgiving timelines. Production doesn't work that way. Data is messy, inputs shift, and things break at 2 AM on a Friday.

**The failure isn't usually the model.** It's the architecture around the model — no scope boundaries, no handoff protocols, no monitoring. An agent that works perfectly 95% of the time and fails silently 5% of the time is worse than not having the agent at all.

**"We'll fix that in production" doesn't happen.** The technical debt from a rushed pilot compounds faster than most teams expect. What took 2 weeks to build takes 6 weeks to stabilize.

**The 90-day calibration is real.** Every agent we've run in production looked different at month 3 than it did at month 1. Not because we changed the model — because the context changed and we hadn't built for drift.

The teams that reach production share one trait: they planned for failure before they launched, not after.

What's the gap between your pilot and production? It's probably not what you think.
