## Draft

We've been running a team of five AI agents in production for three months. Here's what we'd do differently if we started today.

**We'd define failure modes before we defined success metrics.**

Our content agent (we call her Piper) ran perfectly for two weeks. Then she didn't run at all for six days during the busiest inquiry window of the season — and we found out the same way you find out your car has been running on fumes: after the damage was done.

The problem wasn't the agent. The problem was we built her to succeed without building any scaffolding for what "something's wrong" looked like. No alerting, no monitoring, no backup protocol.

We fixed it. But fixing it after six days of missed work cost more than building it right the first time would have.

**Three things we'd add from day one:**
- A daily health check endpoint for every agent that touches time-sensitive work
- An explicit "dead man's switch" — if the agent hasn't produced output by 10 AM, something pings us
- A backup protocol written before launch, not after the first incident

The agents themselves are surprisingly reliable. The infrastructure around them matters as much as the agents do.

If you're building AI agent workflows for your business — whether you're doing it yourself or working with a team like ours — build the monitoring layer first. The agents can wait a week. Your customers can't.

What failure mode have you caught late that you'd wire in from the start next time?
