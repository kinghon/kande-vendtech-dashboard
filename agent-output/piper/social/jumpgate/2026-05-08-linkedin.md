## Post

Deloitte Insights called it out in December:

> "Many so-called agentic initiatives are actually automation use cases in disguise. Enterprises often apply agents where simpler tools would suffice, resulting in poor ROI."

We've seen this firsthand. Eight months of building production AI systems — and the most expensive mistakes weren't technical. They were definitional.

**Automation handles what you already know.** Scripted steps. Deterministic inputs. If A then B. A well-built n8n flow, a Zapier sequence, a scheduled Python script — these are often the right answer. Cheaper, faster to debug, easier to hand off.

**Agents handle what changes.** Situations where the next step depends on what just happened, where failure modes need interpretation, where the job requires judgment that can't be pre-scripted. That's a fundamentally different design pattern — and a fundamentally different failure surface.

The companies getting burned right now are the ones who went to an AI vendor, heard "agentic AI," and built a $200K automation in disguise. When it breaks, it breaks in ways they didn't plan for, because they designed for certainty and deployed into ambiguity.

Here's the test we use before building anything:
1. Does the next step depend on reasoning about what just happened?
2. Does the task change mid-execution based on real-world feedback?
3. Does it need to handle failure modes that can't be enumerated in advance?

If all three are no — it's automation. Build it simply.
If any are yes — now you're talking about an agent. Build it differently.

Eight months of production systems later, we can usually tell in a 20-minute conversation which one you need. Sometimes the answer is: neither yet.

What pattern are you seeing in your organization — real agents, or automation with a better name?
