## Draft

Forbes ran a piece this year: "1.3 Billion AI Agents Are Coming. Most Have No Kill Switch."

The stat came from Google Cloud's 2026 AI Agent Trends Report. The framing was alarming. The reality behind it is simpler than it sounds.

Most agents don't have a kill switch because nobody designed one in. They built the agent to do a task, shipped it, and moved on. The governance layer was never part of the architecture — it was going to be "added later."

Later never comes.

We've been running a multi-agent production system for a while now — Scout, Relay, Piper, Ralph. Each one has an explicit boundary for when it stops and a human reviews. Scout doesn't contact the same lead twice without a check. Relay flags anything outside normal parameters before acting. Piper doesn't send, it drafts. Ralph doesn't deploy without a confirmation gate.

These aren't hard to build. They're just easy to skip when you're moving fast.

The kill switch isn't a safety feature you bolt on at the end. It's an architectural decision you make on day one: what does this agent do when it's uncertain? What does it do when something breaks? Who gets the alert and how fast?

If you're deploying agents right now and those questions don't have clear answers, you're in the 85% that never makes it from pilot to production.

What's your governance layer? Have you actually designed it — or is it "we'll add it later"?
