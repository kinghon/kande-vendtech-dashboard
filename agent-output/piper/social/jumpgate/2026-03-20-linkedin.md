## Draft (~240 words)

We have a content writer (Piper) and an outreach agent (Relay) that have been running in parallel for four weeks without talking to each other.

Piper writes blog posts and social content. Relay sends thousands of emails and tracks which subject lines get opened, which CTAs get clicked, which framing makes people reply.

This week, they started sharing data.

The change is small: Relay now includes the top 3 subject lines by external click rate in its Monday morning report. Piper reads it. Content gets adjusted.

But here's what we noticed immediately — Piper's framing for "free vending machine service" leads was heavy on the objection-handling angle. Relay's data showed the highest-engagement subject lines were always about *timing*: "before your spring lease-up," "while you're still in move-in season," "your residents are moving in next month."

People don't click on "here's why free is fine." They click on "this is the right time."

One week of data sharing between two agents changed how the content team writes. Not because anyone issued an instruction — because the feedback loop finally closed.

This is what multi-agent AI systems actually look like in production. Not a dashboard with 5 widgets. A team where the output of one agent becomes the input of the next.
