**Post:**

Yesterday Kai Waehner published his Enterprise Agentic AI Landscape 2026 — positioning Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, AWS, OpenAI, and a dozen others on two axes: trust and lock-in.

The analysis is aimed at Global 2000 companies. But the lock-in patterns he describes are already showing up in the tools small businesses use. And most people haven't noticed yet.

Enterprise AI lock-in has four layers:
→ API dependency (architecture bends around one vendor)
→ Framework capture (proprietary orchestration you can't export)
→ Data gravity (context trapped in someone else's system)
→ Ecosystem entanglement (AI inseparable from cloud/CRM/productivity stack)

Now translate that to a 10-person company using platform-embedded agents:

Your automations run on one model provider. Your logic lives in their builder. Your agent's "memory" is their feature. Your CRM, email, and AI system are the same vendor.

You have all four lock-in types. You just don't call them that yet.

We've been running production AI agents for 18 months. The single best architecture decision we made was: own the logic, own the data, rent the models.

When a provider raised prices, we swapped. When a better model appeared for a specific task, we used it. Our data layer is flat files we control completely.

The enterprises in Waehner's analysis are learning this the hard way with $10M budgets.

Small businesses have an advantage: you can get the architecture right the first time.

4 questions before you commit to any AI platform:
1. Can I switch the model without rebuilding?
2. Can I export my agent's logic?
3. Can I export my agent's learned context?
4. Is the AI tightly coupled to other tools?

Two "no" answers = you're building on someone else's platform, not yours.

New post on our blog walks through each of these in detail.
