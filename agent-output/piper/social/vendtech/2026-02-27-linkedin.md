Stopped by an apartment complex in Henderson on Tuesday.

The property manager showed me their current vending machine — an old unit wedged in the corner near the back of the mail room. One row of chips was half-stocked. The card reader had an "out of service" sticker on it. She said it had been there for two weeks.

"We've called three times," she told me. "They said someone's coming."

That's a real cost. Not just lost revenue — though that matters — but a resident experience problem. Someone moves into your building, sees a broken machine in the lobby on day one, and it sets a tone.

We walked the property together. The mail room was actually high-traffic. Right next to the elevator bank. There was space for a larger unit with a better product mix — fresh drinks, better water options, a few snack categories her residents had been asking for.

We can have a new machine installed and stocked within a week of signing. No cost to the property. We own and maintain the machine. She gets a commission on every sale.

At the end of the walk, she said: "I didn't realize it was this simple."

It usually is. The complexity is mostly in the old model — operators who treat every property like one of 800 stops on a route, where your building gets serviced when they get around to it.
