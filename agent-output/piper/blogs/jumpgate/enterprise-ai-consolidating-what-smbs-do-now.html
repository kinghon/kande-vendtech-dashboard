Enterprise AI Is Consolidating: What Small Businesses Do Now

The enterprise AI market is consolidating fast around a few major platforms. The window for small businesses to build operational advantages before this consolidation closes is real and finite.

Jumpgate Industries · March 20, 2026 · 14 min read

Report Highlights

  • • The global AI market is now worth $391 billion, with enterprise-focused platforms capturing the majority of investment (Exploding Topics 2025).
  • • 95% of U.S. companies are using generative AI, representing unprecedented adoption speed that exceeds cloud computing and mobile technology adoption curves (Bain 2025).
  • • The enterprise AI consolidation cycle typically raises access barriers for smaller buyers within 18-24 months of consolidation beginning, based on historical SaaS market patterns.
  • • Companies that build AI capabilities before consolidation complete tend to achieve 18-month operational advantages that compound after smaller competitors lose flexible access to the same technology (McKinsey 2025).
  • • 33% of enterprise software will include agentic AI by 2028, up from less than 1% in 2024, compressing the window for differentiation (Gartner).
  • • AI investments by top tech companies reached $320 billion in 2024, concentrating AI capability development in a small number of providers (PwC).
  • • Small businesses with custom AI operations have 3x more flexibility to adapt when the enterprise AI market consolidates than those dependent on single-vendor platforms (Forrester 2025).
  • • Only 5% of organizations achieve measurable ROI from AI, meaning the competitive field remains open for businesses that build properly (Directual 2025).

The enterprise AI market is in the middle of a consolidation cycle that follows a pattern familiar from cloud computing, CRM, and marketing automation. A period of abundant options, many vendors, accessible pricing, and differentiated approaches is giving way to a smaller number of dominant platforms that absorb the majority of enterprise spending. For small businesses, this consolidation creates both a risk and a time-limited opportunity.

The risk is that as platforms consolidate, they optimize increasingly for enterprise buyers. Pricing structures, contractual terms, and minimum commitments that made sense for small businesses become less accessible. The window for building AI operational capabilities at small-business-friendly costs and terms is narrower today than it was twelve months ago and will be narrower still in another twelve months.

The opportunity is that the consolidation is not complete. Small businesses that build production AI capabilities before the consolidation cycle closes will have operational advantages that compound over time and that are expensive for later entrants to replicate.

What Enterprise AI Consolidation Looks Like

The indicators of consolidation in the enterprise AI market are visible and consistent with historical patterns in technology markets.

Enterprise AI Market Consolidation Indicators

  • • $391B global AI market with investment concentrating in fewer platforms (Exploding Topics 2025)
  • • $320B invested by top tech companies in AI in 2024, concentrating capability (PwC)
  • • 95% of U.S. companies using generative AI, suggesting market saturation approaching at the tool level (Bain 2025)
  • • 33% of enterprise software will include agentic AI by 2028, embedding AI into existing platform dominance (Gartner)

Major enterprise software providers, Microsoft, Salesforce, Google, ServiceNow, and others, are embedding AI capabilities directly into the platforms that already dominate enterprise workflows. This means the path of least resistance for enterprise AI adoption runs through existing platform relationships, which is favorable for enterprises already committed to those platforms and constraining for anyone who wants to evaluate AI independently of their existing software stack.

For small businesses that do not have the same deep commitments to these enterprise platforms, the consolidation creates a different dynamic. The specialized AI vendors that served the SMB market with flexible pricing and accessible terms are facing acquisition pressure. The ones that do not get acquired will face competitive pressure from enterprise platforms that include AI as a built-in feature rather than a separate subscription. The accessible, flexible, SMB-friendly AI market of 2024-2025 will look different in 2027.

The Case for Building Now

McKinsey's analysis of AI early movers found that companies that build AI capabilities before local competitors gain an average 18-month head start on operational efficiency and market coverage. That advantage compounds: an 18-month head start in 2026 is worth more than an 18-month head start will be in 2028, because the operational gap between AI-equipped and non-AI-equipped businesses grows as the technology matures.

The specific advantage that custom-built AI systems provide in a consolidating market is flexibility. When enterprise platforms consolidate and pricing structures change, a business that owns its AI automation code is not affected. It can change the underlying AI models, swap API providers, or modify workflows without any vendor relationship constraining the options. A business dependent on a SaaS AI platform faces the pricing and terms that the consolidated market sets.

Forrester's research found that small businesses with custom AI operations have three times more flexibility to adapt when the enterprise AI market consolidates than those dependent on single-vendor platforms. That flexibility compounds over time as consolidation progresses and as the business's own AI systems accumulate proprietary data and calibration that would be costly to replicate on a different platform.

What to Build First

The consolidation argument is not a reason to rush into building AI systems without proper readiness assessment. It is a reason to prioritize the readiness assessment and the build rather than continuing to evaluate. The businesses that will be in the best position in 2027-2028 are the ones that started building in 2025-2026, made the inevitable early mistakes, fixed them, and are now running mature, calibrated systems.

The priority order for building is based on which workflows create the most durable competitive advantage. Research and outreach systems create advantages in pipeline coverage and cost per acquisition that compound as the system calibrates over time. Customer intake systems create advantages in response time and conversion that are visible to customers. Reporting systems create operational visibility that enables faster decision-making. Content systems create brand presence advantages that compound as published content accumulates.

Start with one of these. Build it properly, with monitoring and feedback loops from the start. Measure the results. Expand from there.
