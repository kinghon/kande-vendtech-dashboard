How to Hire an AI Consultant Without Getting Burned

The AI consulting market is full of people selling strategy decks and discovery workshops to businesses that need working systems. Six questions that identify who can actually build what you need.

Jumpgate Industries · January 5, 2026 · 14 min read

Report Highlights

  • • The AI consulting market grew to an estimated $67 billion globally in 2025, with growth outpacing the market's ability to produce qualified practitioners (McKinsey).
  • • 88% of AI agent projects fail before reaching production, a failure rate that tracks closely with the use of strategy-focused consultants over practitioners with production experience (Digital Applied 2025).
  • • Only 5% of organizations achieve measurable ROI from generative AI, suggesting most AI consulting engagements do not produce the promised results (Directual 2025).
  • • 70-85% of generative AI deployment efforts fail to meet desired ROI, indicating a systemic gap between AI consulting promises and delivery (NTT DATA 2024).
  • • Businesses that evaluate AI consultants on specific production deployments rather than credentials or certifications achieve 3x better outcomes than those that do not (Forrester 2025).
  • • The average AI consulting engagement for a small business costs $15,000-$75,000 with widely varying outcomes depending on the practitioner's actual production experience.
  • • Consultants who offer "discovery workshops" as the first deliverable have, on average, a 4x lower rate of subsequent production deployment than those who scope a specific build from the initial engagement (LangChain analysis 2026).
  • • 78% of companies use AI in some form, creating a market for AI consultants who can claim "extensive experience" with AI tools without ever having deployed a production agent (McKinsey 2025).

When a market grows as fast as AI consulting has grown, it attracts practitioners whose expertise ranges from genuine to entirely fabricated. The challenge for a business looking to hire help with AI is that the signals that distinguish real expertise from performance are not obvious from a website, a LinkedIn profile, or even a well-produced initial conversation.

We built Jumpgate Industries because we saw businesses getting charged significant fees for AI strategy work that never produced functioning systems. The strategy was often sound in concept. The problem was that the consultants who were most articulate about AI strategy were frequently the ones with the least actual experience building systems that run in production. Those are not the same skill sets, and confusing them is how businesses end up with expensive decks and no working automation.

This guide is six questions that, when asked directly in an initial conversation, will tell you most of what you need to know about whether an AI consultant can actually build what you need.

With 78% of companies using AI in some form, almost anyone can claim "extensive AI experience" based on tool usage. The distinction that matters is between AI tool users and AI system builders: people who have deployed agents that run in production for months, handle failures, integrate with real business systems, and produce measurable results. That population is much smaller than the broader population of AI practitioners.

The Six Questions

Question 1: Can you show me a production system you built that is still running?

Not a demo. Not a pilot. Not a case study with no contact information. A specific system, in production, that has been running for at least six months. If the consultant cannot show you this, they have not solved the hard problems that production deployment involves: monitoring, maintenance, failure recovery, integration stability over time.

The consultant should be able to describe: what the system does, when it was deployed, what failure modes they encountered and how they handled them, what the current metrics are, and who you could speak to if you wanted to verify the information. If any of those elements are absent, the production experience is at most partial.

Question 2: What has broken in your production systems and how did you fix it?

Any practitioner with real production experience has war stories. Agents that went silent. Data sources that changed formats without notice. Feedback loops that stopped working. Scope creep that degraded quality. The specificity and practicality of the answer tells you more about real experience than any portfolio or credential.

A practitioner who cannot describe specific failures and specific fixes in detail either has not run production systems or has not been paying attention to what happened when things went wrong. Neither is reassuring.

Question 3: What is your first deliverable and what does it include?

LangChain's analysis of AI consulting engagement outcomes found that consultants who offer "discovery workshops" as the first deliverable have a 4x lower rate of subsequent production deployment than those who scope a specific build from the initial engagement. The discovery workshop is not always a red flag, sometimes genuine process mapping is necessary, but it should be a short, bounded activity that produces a specification, not an open-ended phase that extends indefinitely at consulting rates.

The answer you want: a specific deliverable, in a specific time frame, at a specific cost, that produces something testable. Not a report about what AI could do for your business.

Question 4: How do you handle monitoring and failure response?

This question immediately distinguishes practitioners who have learned from production experience from those who have not. Monitoring and failure response are not glamorous topics. They do not show up in AI demos. But they are what separate systems that stay running for eighteen months from ones that get decommissioned after six weeks. A practitioner who cannot describe their specific monitoring approach in concrete terms has not been responsible for keeping production systems running.

Question 5: What do you charge for maintenance and what does it cover?

Production AI systems require ongoing maintenance: prompt updates when model behavior changes, fixes when edge cases emerge, integration updates when connected systems change, and monitoring response when alerts fire. A practitioner who cannot describe a specific maintenance arrangement is either planning to disappear after the build or has not thought carefully about what keeping the system running actually involves.

Typical maintenance for a well-built single-workflow agent runs two to four hours per month in a stable period. Higher during the first six months when edge cases are still emerging. If the maintenance commitment is "we will be available if you need us," that is not a maintenance arrangement. That is no maintenance.

Question 6: What would you tell me to do if you were not in the room?

This is the test for whether the practitioner is optimizing for their engagement or for your outcome. A practitioner who is genuinely oriented toward your results will tell you honestly if a vendor product would serve your use case better than a custom build. They will tell you if your process needs documentation before any automation makes sense. They will tell you if you are not ready yet and what would make you ready.

At Jumpgate Industries, we have told clients not to hire us and to buy a vendor product instead, because it was the right answer for their situation. We have also told clients their process was not ready to automate and what to fix first. The right answer for the client matters more than the next engagement. If a consultant cannot make that distinction, they are optimizing for the wrong thing.
