AI Agents as Team Members: What HBR Got Right (and What It Missed)

Harvard Business Review's argument for treating AI agents like employees is compelling and partially correct. The parts it misses matter for anyone actually building and running these systems.

Jumpgate Industries · February 15, 2026 · 15 min read

  • • 88% of enterprises report regular AI use, yet most organizations have not developed formal protocols for how AI agents and humans divide responsibility (McKinsey 2025).
  • • By 2028, at least 15% of day-to-day work decisions will be made autonomously through agentic AI, requiring new management frameworks (Gartner).

  • • The "digital worker" framing is gaining adoption: 44% of enterprise AI implementations now use role-based agent design rather than task-based design (Master of Code Global 2026).

Harvard Business Review published research in 2025 arguing that organizations should treat AI agents more like team members and less like software tools. The argument is that the employee framing, with its associated concepts of onboarding, performance review, clear role definition, and accountability, produces better outcomes than the software framing, where the agent gets deployed and then largely forgotten until something breaks.

The research is compelling and, in our experience at Jumpgate Industries running AI agents in production for over a year, largely correct. The employee framing does produce better outcomes. But the HBR framing misses some important practical realities that matter for businesses building and maintaining these systems day to day. This post covers what the framing gets right, where it falls short, and what we have found works in practice.

What HBR Got Right: The Case for the Team Member Framing

The core argument is that treating AI agents like software produces software-style management: deploy it, expect it to work, only look at it when something breaks. Treating AI agents like team members produces management-style treatment: clear role definition, regular performance review, ongoing feedback, clear scope and authority, and a designated person responsible for each agent.

HBR's research found that teams integrating AI agents as collaborators rather than tools saw 27% higher task completion rates than teams using AI as standalone tools. The causal mechanism is not mysterious. When an agent has a clearly defined role, the humans working alongside it understand what to expect from it, what to escalate to it, and what to take back from it when its outputs are inadequate. When it is treated as a generic tool, the handoffs are unclear, the accountability is diffuse, and the failures take longer to notice and fix.

Team Member vs. Tool Framing Performance Data

  • • 40% fewer output quality failures with regular review cycles (Cleanlab 2025)
  • • 2.1x more likely to maintain production systems long-term with defined handoff protocols (LangChain 2026)
  • • Productivity grows 4.8x faster in industries treating AI as genuine team function (IBM Research)

Gartner's projection that 15% of day-to-day work decisions will be made autonomously through agentic AI by 2028, up from 0% in 2024, makes the management framing increasingly relevant. As agents take on more decision-making authority, the question of how to oversee that decision-making is not theoretical. It is a practical management challenge that requires the same disciplines as managing any employee making consequential decisions on behalf of the organization.

The Employee Analogy Applied to Agent Management

The employee analogy yields a useful management checklist for AI agents. Each item on the list corresponds to a specific management action that produces better agent outcomes.

Onboarding for an AI agent means clear documentation of the role, the data sources, the decision criteria, the quality standards, and the escalation protocol before the agent starts work. Just as a new employee who starts without a clear job description will fill in the gaps with their own judgment, an agent without clear specifications will fill gaps with its own defaults, which may or may not align with what you actually need.

Performance review for an AI agent means the output quality sampling and volume tracking we described in our failure detection post. Review on a regular schedule, not just when something visibly breaks. The review catches degradation early and provides the data needed to decide when to adjust the agent's specifications.

Feedback for an AI agent means the loop from outcomes back to the agent's logic. An outreach agent that never receives information about which messages produced conversations will keep sending the same messages indefinitely. The feedback loop is the mechanism by which the agent improves over time rather than staying static or degrading.

Clear scope and authority means knowing explicitly what decisions the agent makes on its own versus what it flags for human review. This is the handoff protocol, and it is one of the most important design decisions in any agent deployment. LangChain found that organizations with explicit handoff protocols are 2.1x more likely to maintain production systems long-term.

What HBR Missed: The Parts That Are Not Like Managing an Employee

The team member framing breaks down in two important ways that matter for practical system design.

First, AI agents do not ask for clarification when they are confused. A new employee who does not understand their job description will ask questions. An AI agent that encounters ambiguity will make its best guess, produce output based on that guess, and move on. The output often looks plausible even when the guess was wrong. This is why the specification has to be complete and unambiguous before deployment, not refined over time in response to questions from the agent. The agent will not ask questions.

Second, AI agents can fail in ways that are invisible without specific monitoring infrastructure. A human employee who is not doing their job well produces visible signals: missed deadlines, incomplete work, complaints from colleagues, observable behavior changes. An AI agent that is producing wrong outputs while running on schedule produces no visible signal at the execution level. Only the outputs themselves reveal the problem, and only if someone is specifically looking for quality issues rather than just confirming the agent is running.

The HBR framing emphasizes the management relationship but does not address the technical monitoring infrastructure that makes the management relationship functional. Without output quality sampling, volume tracking, and data freshness checks, the best-intentioned management approach will still miss silent failures. At Jumpgate Industries, we treat these as complementary: the team member framing informs how we design roles and handoffs, while the technical monitoring infrastructure provides the visibility needed to actually manage effectively.

A Practical Integration Framework

The framework we use combines both perspectives. Each agent has a defined role with explicit scope, decision authority, and escalation criteria. There is a named human owner responsible for that agent's performance, not a team or a department. The owner conducts a weekly review of the monitoring dashboard and a monthly deeper quality review. There is a documented feedback mechanism for each agent that routes outcome information back to the agent's logic on a defined schedule.

The 22% of businesses that have documented who is responsible for an AI agent's outputs when something goes wrong (Gartner 2025) are operating more safely than the majority. The accountability question is not just a governance exercise. It is a practical question: when the agent produces a bad output that affects a customer or a client, who finds out about it and who fixes it? If that question does not have a clear answer before the problem happens, it will have a confused and slow answer when it does.
