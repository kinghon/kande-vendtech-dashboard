The "Agentic" Label Went Mainstream. Production Did Not.

Every software product now calls itself agentic. Less than 10% of organizations have actually scaled AI agents in any single function. The gap between the label and the reality is worth understanding before you buy into either.

Jumpgate Industries · March 1, 2026 · 13 min read

Report Highlights

  • • Less than 10% of organizations have scaled AI agents in any individual function, despite the word "agentic" appearing on thousands of software product pages (McKinsey State of AI 2025).
  • • 88% of AI agent projects fail before reaching production (Digital Applied 2025).
  • • Gartner identifies agentic AI as a top strategic trend for 2025-2026, but notes 33% enterprise software penetration is the 2028 target, not the 2025 reality.
  • • 78% of companies use AI in some form, but most use AI tools rather than production agents (McKinsey 2025).
  • • The agentic AI market is currently at $5.43 billion, less than 2% of the total AI market, reflecting the gap between AI adoption broadly and agentic AI specifically (Precedence Research).
  • • Only 5% of organizations achieve measurable ROI from generative AI investments, suggesting most "agentic" implementations are not producing the outcomes the label implies (Directual 2025).
  • • Vendors that use "agentic" primarily as a marketing term tend to have 4x lower production deployment rates compared to vendors that define agentic capability with specific technical criteria (LangChain 2026).
  • • The production gap will close: Gartner projects 33% of enterprise software will include real agentic AI by 2028, a 33-fold increase from the less than 1% in 2024.

Somewhere in 2025, every software company discovered the word "agentic." CRM platforms became agentic CRMs. Marketing tools became agentic marketing platforms. Customer support software became agentic support. The word appeared on product pages, in press releases, at conferences, and in investor decks at a pace that suggests it was added to a vocabulary list somewhere and distributed industry-wide.

Meanwhile, McKinsey's State of AI 2025 found that less than 10% of organizations have scaled AI agents in any individual function. The word is everywhere. The thing it is supposed to describe is extremely rare.

This is not new in technology. The same pattern played out with "cloud-native," "AI-powered," "machine learning-driven," and before all of those, with "intelligent" software of various types. What makes the agentic version worth examining is that the gap between the label and the reality is creating real confusion for businesses trying to make informed decisions about what to build or buy.

What "Agentic" Is Supposed to Mean

Gartner's definition of agentic AI is specific: systems capable of making decisions and taking actions autonomously to achieve goals without requiring human approval at each step. The key elements are goal orientation, autonomous decision-making, and execution that does not wait for human intervention. A system that presents options for a human to choose from is not agentic. A system that makes the choice and executes it is.

The Label vs. Reality Gap

  • • Thousands of software products now use "agentic" in their marketing
  • • Less than 10% of organizations have scaled actual AI agents (McKinsey 2025)
  • • Agentic AI market: $5.43B vs. total AI market $391B = less than 2% (Precedence Research vs. Exploding Topics)
  • • Only 5% achieve measurable ROI from AI investments (Directual 2025)

By that definition, most of what is being sold as "agentic" in 2026 is not agentic. A CRM that uses AI to suggest what email to send next is AI-assisted, not agentic. A support platform that uses AI to generate suggested responses for a human to approve is AI-powered, not agentic. These are useful capabilities and real improvements over previous versions of the same tools. They are not the same as an agent that monitors your prospect list, identifies the optimal moment to reach out based on behavioral signals, composes a message, and sends it without any human intervention in the process.

Why the Label Proliferated

The label proliferated for the same reason every previous technology buzzword proliferated: it generated positive attention at conferences, in press coverage, and in investor conversations. Adding "agentic" to a product description costs nothing and creates positive associations with a technology category that is genuinely exciting. The penalty for misusing it is low because the buyers evaluating the products often do not have the technical background to test whether the label is accurate.

LangChain's 2026 analysis found that vendors using "agentic" primarily as a marketing term have four times lower production deployment rates compared to vendors that define agentic capability with specific technical criteria. The market is beginning to self-correct as buyers become more sophisticated, but the correction takes time.

What to Ask When Evaluating "Agentic" Products

Three questions cut through the label to the substance. First: what specific decisions does the system make autonomously, and what specific actions does it execute without human approval? If the answer is vague or describes suggestions rather than actions, the system is AI-assisted, not agentic.

Second: what does the human handoff look like, and under what conditions does the system escalate to a human rather than acting independently? An honest answer to this question reveals whether the system has genuine autonomous execution capability or is primarily presenting options for human review.

Third: can you show me a production deployment where the system has been running autonomously for six months or more? Real agentic systems have production histories. Products that are labeled agentic primarily for marketing purposes often cannot demonstrate extended autonomous operation.
