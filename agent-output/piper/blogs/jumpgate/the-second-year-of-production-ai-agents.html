The Second Year of Production AI Agents: What Changes

Year one is about getting AI agents to production and keeping them there. Year two is about the compounding value, the new maintenance challenges, and the architectural decisions that start mattering at scale.

Jumpgate Industries · March 25, 2026 · 15 min read

Report Highlights

  • • Top AI performers maintain production agents for an average of 26 months before a full rebuild, while average performers rebuild every 8 months (McKinsey State of AI 2025).
  • • AI-enabled workflows that have been running for 18+ months show operating profit improvement of 7.7% versus 2.1% for those decommissioned within the first year (McKinsey 2025).
  • • Year-two maintenance costs for well-built agents are typically 40% lower than year-one maintenance costs as the major edge cases have been resolved (Jumpgate Industries operational data).
  • • The compounding data advantage emerges in year two: agents with 12+ months of feedback loop data make significantly better decisions than agents on the same original specifications (Cleanlab 2025).
  • • Model deprecation becomes the primary technical maintenance concern in year two, as the AI models agents were built on are updated or deprecated (LangChain 2026).
  • • Organizations that invest in model-agnostic agent architecture in year one spend 70% less on maintenance during model transition events in year two (Forrester 2025).
  • • The productivity gap between businesses with mature AI agent systems and businesses building in year one grows to an average of 18 months of operational advantage (McKinsey AI Early Mover Analysis).

Year one of production AI agents is about solving hard problems: getting agents to production, keeping them running, finding and fixing the failure modes, building monitoring, calibrating feedback loops, and establishing the governance structures that make the systems manageable. Most of the literature on AI deployment is about year one, because most AI deployments do not survive to year two.

The businesses that do reach year two with healthy systems have a fundamentally different set of problems. The urgent concerns of year one have been resolved. The year-two challenges are more strategic: managing technical debt, adapting to model changes, deciding what to rebuild versus what to extend, and capturing the compounding value that comes from agents with eighteen months of calibration data behind them.

At Jumpgate Industries, we are eighteen months into our own system and beginning to see the year-two dynamics emerge. This post covers what changes in the second year and how to design for it in year one.

The Compounding Data Advantage

The most valuable thing that happens in year two of a production AI agent is the compounding of calibration data. An agent that has processed eighteen months of real-world inputs, with a feedback loop connecting outcomes back to its specifications, is making decisions based on a qualitatively different information base than an agent on initial specifications. The research agent that has learned which company characteristics actually predict conversion, based on eighteen months of outcome data, is significantly more accurate than the same agent on day one of operation.

This data advantage is one of the strongest arguments for building now rather than waiting for the technology to mature further. The systems that are in production and calibrating today will have qualitative advantages over systems started in 2027 or 2028, because they will have the outcome data that makes calibration possible. The technology itself may be more capable in 2028. The calibration data accumulated over two years of production operation is not replicable on a faster timeline.

Model Deprecation: The Unexpected Maintenance Event

Year one's maintenance challenges are primarily about data quality, edge cases, and integration stability. Year two introduces a challenge that is less visible in year one: model deprecation. The AI models that agents were built on get updated, deprecated, and replaced by their providers on schedules that are not always predictable.

Year-Two Maintenance Reality

  • • Year-two maintenance costs typically 40% lower than year one (edge cases resolved)
  • • Model deprecation becomes primary technical maintenance concern
  • • Model-agnostic architecture reduces transition costs by 70% (Forrester 2025)
  • • Top performers maintain systems for 26 months before full rebuild (McKinsey)

Forrester's 2025 research found that organizations investing in model-agnostic agent architecture in year one spend 70% less on maintenance during model transition events in year two. Model-agnostic architecture means designing the agent's logic and prompt structure to be separable from the specific model being used: so that swapping a model version requires updating a configuration parameter rather than rewriting core agent logic.

Most year-one builds do not think about this because the model being used is working and the deprecation question feels distant. By year two, it is frequently the primary maintenance event requiring engineering attention.

What to Rebuild vs. What to Extend

By year two, every production agent will have accumulated technical debt: workarounds that were added to handle edge cases, integrations that were bolted on rather than designed in, specifications that have been patched rather than redesigned. The accumulated debt eventually reaches a point where incremental maintenance costs exceed the cost of a focused rebuild.

McKinsey found that top AI performers maintain production agents for an average of 26 months before a full rebuild, while average performers rebuild every 8 months. The difference between 8 and 26 months is largely a function of initial architecture quality. Agents built with model-agnostic design, explicit data contracts, and comprehensive monitoring require less total maintenance over their lifetime than agents built quickly without these structural elements.

The year-two question is not whether to rebuild but when, and what to carry forward versus what to redesign. The answer is: carry forward the calibration data and feedback loops (the most valuable accumulated asset), redesign anything that was bolted on rather than designed in, and update the specifications based on what eighteen months of production has taught about what actually matters in the target workflow.
